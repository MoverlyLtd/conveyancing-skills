# Client Money Obligations Under CLC Regulation

As a licensed conveyancer regulated by the CLC, your obligations around client money derive from two areas of the CLC Handbook: the **Code of Conduct** (Principle 3) and the **Accounts Code**.

## Code of Conduct — Principle 3: Act in the Best Interests of Clients

Outcome **3.4** requires you to **protect client money and assets**. This is an overarching ethical obligation — you must ensure that any money or property entrusted to you by a client is safeguarded at all times. Evidence of compliance includes demonstrating adherence to the Accounts Code (below).

## Accounts Code — Specific Requirements

The CLC's Accounts Code sets out four key requirements for handling client money:

| Ref | Requirement | Detail |
|-----|-------------|--------|
| **ACC1** | **Client money must be held in a designated client account** | All money received on behalf of a client must be paid into a separate, designated client account — never mixed with the firm's own money (office account). This is an ongoing obligation for the entire duration you hold client funds. |
| **ACC2** | **Regular reconciliation of the client account** | You must reconcile your client account at least **monthly**. This means checking that the balance held in the bank matches the total of individual client ledger balances. Discrepancies must be investigated and resolved promptly. |
| **ACC3** | **Interest on client money** | You must handle interest earned on client money in accordance with what was agreed in your **terms of engagement**. You should address interest arrangements when you first receive funds — clients are generally entitled to a fair rate of interest on money held for any significant period. |
| **ACC4** | **Accountant's report** | You must submit an **annual accountant's report** to the CLC. This is prepared by a qualified accountant who examines your client account records and confirms (or flags issues with) your compliance with the Accounts Code. |

## Related Obligations That Touch Client Money

Several other CLC requirements interact with client money handling:

- **AML3 — Source of funds verification**: Before accepting any funds into the client account, you must verify the source of those funds. This is an anti-money laundering obligation that directly gates when and whether client money can be received.

- **AML4 — Source of wealth verification**: Where the risk profile requires it, you must also verify the client's source of wealth before completion — particularly relevant for large sums passing through your client account.

- **ETE1–ETE2 — Terms of engagement and cost estimates**: Your written engagement terms (issued at instruction) must clearly set out how costs, disbursements, and interest on client money will be handled. This is foundational — ACC3 compliance depends on having clear terms in place.

- **ETE4 — Final billing**: You must issue a final bill promptly after completion, which triggers the obligation to return any residual client money held on account.

## Key Differences from SRA-Regulated Firms

The CLC takes an **outcomes-focused** approach. Unlike the SRA Accounts Rules (which are highly prescriptive about specific procedures), the CLC requires you to deliver the outcomes above but gives you flexibility in how you achieve them. That said, the core protections are substantively similar:

- Client money must be segregated
- Accounts must be reconciled regularly
- Independent accountant oversight is mandatory
- Interest arrangements must be transparent

## Practical Summary

In day-to-day practice, your client money obligations boil down to:

1. **Segregate** — always hold client money in a designated client account, separate from office money
2. **Reconcile** — check balances monthly at minimum
3. **Be transparent** — agree interest and cost arrangements in your terms of engagement upfront
4. **Verify** — confirm source of funds before accepting money (AML obligations)
5. **Account** — submit your annual accountant's report to the CLC
6. **Return** — bill promptly after completion and return any surplus funds

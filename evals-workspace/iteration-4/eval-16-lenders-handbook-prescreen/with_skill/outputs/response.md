# Lender Lease Term Assessment: 72 Years Remaining

## Question
Will Halifax lend on a property with 72 years remaining on the lease? What about Nationwide and Barclays?

---

## Halifax

**Answer: Yes — 72 years is acceptable.**

Per Halifax's Part 2 response to section 5.14.1:

> *"Minimum 70 years from the date of the mortgage."*

With 72 years remaining, the property exceeds Halifax's 70-year minimum by 2 years. No referral to the lender is required on lease length alone.

**Handbook reference:** UK Finance Lender's Handbook Part 2, Halifax, section 5.14.1.

---

## Nationwide Building Society

**Answer: It depends on the LTV and property type — potentially acceptable, but with conditions.**

Nationwide's Part 2 response to section 5.14.1 sets out a more nuanced position:

> *"Our minimum unexpired lease term is 55 years, except where lending is over 85% of the purchase price/valuation on a second hand flat, in which case our minimum unexpired term is 90 years."*
>
> *"There must be at least 30 years remaining at the end of the mortgage term (regardless of the length of lease at the start)."*

At 72 years remaining:

- **If LTV is 85% or below:** 72 years exceeds the 55-year minimum — **acceptable** (subject to the 30-year end-of-term requirement).
- **If LTV is over 85% on a second-hand flat:** 72 years is below the 90-year minimum — **not acceptable**.
- **End-of-term check:** For a typical 25-year mortgage, 72 − 25 = 47 years remaining at end of term, which exceeds the 30-year requirement. For a 40-year term, 72 − 40 = 32 years — still just above the 30-year threshold.

Additionally, if the unexpired lease term differs from what is stated on the mortgage offer, Nationwide has specific reporting requirements depending on whether the offer term was above or below 85 years.

**Handbook reference:** UK Finance Lender's Handbook Part 2, Nationwide Building Society, section 5.14.1.

---

## Barclays Bank UK PLC

**Answer: No — 72 years is above 70 but Barclays requires a minimum of 70 years, with very limited exceptions.**

Per Barclays' Part 2 response to section 5.14.1:

> *"Leases with less than 70 years at the commencement of the mortgage are not acceptable."*
>
> *"Leases with fewer than 70 years should only be referred to the issuing office where the following scenario applies, as discretion may be applied subject to bank approval:*
> - *Property is located in any of the following prestigious developments: Cadogan, Crown, Grosvenor, Howard de Walden, Portman or Wellcome Trust Estates in Central London AND*
> - *The value of the property subject to the short remaining term is £500,000 or more AND*
> - *The loan to value does not exceed 90% for purchases…"*

At 72 years remaining, the property **exceeds** Barclays' 70-year minimum threshold. **72 years is acceptable** — the property does not fall into the sub-70-year category that triggers a refusal or the exceptional referral route for prestigious Central London estates.

**Handbook reference:** UK Finance Lender's Handbook Part 2, Barclays Bank UK PLC, section 5.14.1.

## Practical Advice

While 72 years clears the minimum for Halifax and Barclays (and Nationwide at lower LTVs), it is a relatively short lease that is approaching the danger zone. The conveyancer should:

1. **Advise the borrower** that a short lease depreciates in value and will become increasingly difficult to remortgage or sell
2. **Investigate lease extension prospects** — the borrower may qualify for a statutory lease extension under the Leasehold Reform, Housing and Urban Development Act 1993 (as amended by the Leasehold Reform (Ground Rents) Act 2022)
3. **Check the mortgage offer** to confirm the lease term stated matches reality, and report any discrepancy per the lender's Part 2 instructions
4. **Consider future remortgaging** — if the borrower takes a 25-year term, the lease will have only ~47 years remaining at maturity, which would be below most lenders' minimums for a new mortgage at that point

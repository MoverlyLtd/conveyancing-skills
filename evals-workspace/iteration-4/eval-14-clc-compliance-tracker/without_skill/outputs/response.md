# CLC Requirements for Handling Client Complaints

As a CLC-regulated firm, you have specific obligations under the CLC's regulatory framework when handling client complaints. Here's what you need to do:

## Immediate Steps

1. **Acknowledge the complaint promptly** — The CLC expects you to acknowledge receipt of the complaint in writing, typically within a few working days.

2. **Record the complaint** — Log it in your firm's complaints register. The CLC requires firms to maintain proper records of all complaints received.

3. **Designate a handler** — Ensure the complaint is handled by a senior person in the firm, ideally someone not directly involved in the matter being complained about.

## Your Complaints Procedure

The CLC's Code of Conduct requires every regulated firm to have a written complaints procedure. Your handling must follow this procedure, which should include:

- **Clear timescales** for investigating and responding to the complaint
- **A substantive response** — Investigate the complaint thoroughly and provide a detailed written response, typically within 8 weeks
- **Inform the client of their right to escalate** — If the client remains dissatisfied after your final response (or if 8 weeks pass without resolution), you must inform them of their right to refer the complaint to the **Legal Ombudsman (LeO)**

## CLC Code of Conduct Obligations

The CLC's outcomes-focused regulatory framework requires you to:

- **Act in clients' best interests** — The complaint itself may indicate a failure here that needs addressing
- **Provide a proper standard of service** — Delays in conveyancing can breach this core outcome
- **Communicate effectively** — Keep the client informed about the progress of their complaint (and the underlying transaction)
- **Act with integrity** — Handle the complaint honestly and fairly, even if it reflects poorly on your firm

## Regarding the Delay Specifically

Since the complaint concerns delays, you should also:

- **Review the file** to understand what caused the delays and whether they were within your control
- **Update the client** on the current status of their conveyancing matter and provide realistic timescales going forward
- **Consider whether compensation or a fee reduction is appropriate** if the delays were your fault

## Record-Keeping and Reporting

- Maintain full records of the complaint and your response
- The CLC may ask about complaint volumes and outcomes during inspections or as part of regulatory returns
- Monitor for patterns — if delay complaints are recurring, this may indicate a systemic issue the CLC would expect you to address proactively

## Legal Ombudsman Signposting

You **must** inform the client that if they are not satisfied with your response, they can refer their complaint to:

- **The Legal Ombudsman** (LeO)
- There are time limits for referral to LeO (generally within 6 months of your final response, and within 6 years of the act/omission or 3 years from when the client should reasonably have known about it)
- Provide LeO's contact details in your final response

## Key Takeaway

The CLC takes a proportionate, outcomes-focused approach to regulation. They want to see that you have robust procedures in place, that you handle complaints fairly and promptly, and that clients are properly informed of their options. A well-handled complaint — even one that identifies genuine failings — demonstrates good regulatory compliance. Failing to have or follow a complaints procedure is itself a regulatory breach.

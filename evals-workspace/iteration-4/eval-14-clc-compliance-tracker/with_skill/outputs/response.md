# CLC Requirements for Handling a Client Complaint About Delays

As a CLC-regulated firm, receiving a client complaint about delays in your conveyancing service triggers obligations under several of the CLC's Ethical Principles. Here's what you need to do:

## 1. Handle the Complaint Fairly and Promptly (Principle 3, Outcome 3.6)

This is your primary obligation. The CLC requires you to:

- **Log the complaint** in your firm's complaints register immediately, recording the date received, the client's concerns, and the nature of the complaint (service delays).
- **Acknowledge the complaint promptly** — don't let it sit. Write to the client confirming you've received their complaint and explain your complaints procedure.
- **Investigate and resolve** the complaint fairly. Look into what caused the delays, whether they were within your control, and what can be done to put things right.
- **Document the resolution timeline** — keep records of every step taken to address the complaint.

## 2. Review Whether You've Met Your Service Standards (Principle 2, Outcomes 2.1 & 2.3)

The complaint about delays directly relates to your obligation to provide a **competent, timely and diligent service** (Outcome 2.1). You should:

- **Review the file** to understand what caused the delays — were response times reasonable? Were key dates diarised and monitored (Transaction Files Code, TF2)?
- **Check supervision** — was the matter adequately supervised (Outcome 2.3)? Did the fee earner have appropriate oversight?
- **Assess whether staff competence** was a factor (Outcome 2.2) — was the work allocated to someone with the right skills and capacity?

## 3. Consider Your Client Communication Obligations (Principle 3, Outcomes 3.1 & 3.3)

Delay complaints often stem from poor communication. The CLC requires you to:

- **Keep the client informed of progress** (Outcome 3.3) — review your communication log. Were updates provided regularly? Did the client know what was happening and why?
- **Put client interests at the centre of service delivery** (Outcome 3.1) — consider whether the client's interests were properly prioritised.

## 4. Be Prepared to Cooperate with the Legal Ombudsman (Principle 4, Outcome 4.3)

If the client is not satisfied with your response and escalates to the **Legal Ombudsman (LeO)**:

- You must **cooperate fully** with any LeO investigation.
- Ensure your complaints procedure informs the client of their right to escalate to the Legal Ombudsman if they remain dissatisfied, including the relevant time limits.

## 5. Report to the CLC If Necessary (Principle 4, Outcome 4.2)

If the complaint reveals a systemic issue or a matter that the CLC should know about (e.g., a pattern of service failures, or concerns about an individual's conduct), you have an obligation to **report relevant matters to the CLC without delay**.

## Practical Steps — Summary Checklist

| Step | Action | Timing |
|------|--------|--------|
| 1 | Log complaint in complaints register | Immediately |
| 2 | Acknowledge complaint to client in writing | Within 1-2 working days |
| 3 | Investigate the cause of delays (review file, communications, supervision) | Promptly |
| 4 | Provide substantive response with proposed resolution | Per your complaints procedure (typically within 8 weeks) |
| 5 | Inform client of right to escalate to Legal Ombudsman if dissatisfied | In your response |
| 6 | Review whether systemic issues need reporting to CLC | Ongoing |
| 7 | Update complaints register with outcome | On resolution |

## Key Difference from SRA Firms

Remember — as a CLC-regulated firm, the CLC is **outcomes-focused**. You're not required to follow a prescriptive protocol step-by-step (as SRA/CQS firms might). Instead, you must **deliver the required outcomes**: a fair resolution, proper communication, and documented evidence that you handled the complaint appropriately. How you achieve those outcomes is for your firm to determine through its own procedures.

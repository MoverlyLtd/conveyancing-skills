Under the Conveyancing Association Protocol (5th Edition, 2023), you treat **planning permission** and **building regulations** as **separate checks**, even if the same works are involved (CA **4.0**).

## 1) Planning permission (rear extension; no records)
Apply the CA **4.0 Planning Permission** decision tree:

- First establish whether the extension **required planning permission** (many rear extensions may be permitted development, but you still need to check whether PD applied at the time and whether any limits/Article 4 directions/conditions restrict it).
- If **no planning permission was obtained** and the works were completed in **2021**:
  - That is **within 4 years**, so it’s **not** in the “no further action” category.
  - The protocol approach is that where works are **<12 months old** you look to **regularisation/bespoke solutions**, but for **>12 months and <4 years** the usual protocol outcome is to **obtain an indemnity insurance policy** (CA **4.0**).

Also, the protocol expects you to:
- **Advise the client and the valuer** about the absence of planning permission/records and ask the **valuer/surveyor to confirm the extension is structurally sound** (CA **4.0**).

### Enforcement time-limits you should have in mind (and explain)
When advising on risk, the protocol position also expects you to reference the key enforcement windows:
- **4-year rule**: operational development (e.g., building an extension) / change of use to a single dwelling (if applicable)
- **10-year rule**: breach of condition / other material change of use
- **12-month rule**: **listed building** enforcement (where relevant)

## 2) Building Regulations (separate from planning)
Even if you resolve planning risk (e.g., via indemnity), you must **separately** address building regs for 2021 works under CA **4.0 Building Regulations**:

- 2021 is **post-1984**. If there is **no completion certificate**:
  - If the works are **structural** (a typical extension usually is), the protocol position is to **recommend indemnity at the seller’s cost**, and
  - In all “no certificate” cases: **advise the client and surveyor** that local authority consent/certification is missing and the surveyor should consider safety/compliance implications (CA **4.0**).

(Practically, you’d also consider whether a **regularisation certificate** is feasible/appropriate, but note that approaching the council can affect the availability of indemnity insurance.)

## 3) Communication / speed expectations (to avoid delay)
The protocol’s delay-reduction standards in **CA 1.0** (communication) and the general delay provisions in **CA 1.0–1.6** mean the conveyancers should keep this moving with defined response expectations:
- **Acknowledge correspondence within 2 working days**
- **Respond substantively within 5 working days**
- If a full response isn’t possible, provide **weekly updates** until it is resolved  
(These timeframes are used to keep planning/building-regs queries from stalling the chain—CA **1.0**.)

## 4) If the other side won’t engage / delays persist (escalation)
Where the missing planning/building regs point is causing delay, the protocol escalation steps are:
1) raise with the other firm’s **fee earner** →  
2) escalate to a **senior/manager/partner or compliance** contact →  
3) if still unresolved, consider referring to the relevant **regulatory body** (SRA/CLC), depending on who regulates the firm  
(Protocol approach to managing delays/escalation: CA **1.0**).

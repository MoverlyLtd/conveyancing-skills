For a CQS renewal audit, the assessor is primarily checking that your **firm-level systems, controls and culture** meet the Law Society’s CQS practice management standards and that you can evidence **consistent Conveyancing Protocol adoption** across the practice.

It’s worth separating two things clearly:

- **CQS (Law Society)** = accredits the **firm’s processes and controls** for residential conveyancing.
- **SRA** = regulates the firm and individuals, and sets **individual competence/CPD and conduct** requirements. CQS will expect training and supervision systems, but it is **not** the same as (or a substitute for) SRA competence obligations.

Below are the key areas an assessor typically focuses on, and the evidence you should have ready.

---

## 1) Governance & ownership of CQS / Protocol compliance
**What they check**
- There is a clear CQS governance structure and accountability.
- Someone senior “owns” Protocol compliance and monitoring, and it’s not just informal.

**Have ready**
- Name and details of the **CQS Senior Responsible Officer (SRO)** (and deputies/cover).
- A short written **CQS/Protocol compliance monitoring procedure** (what you check, how often, who signs off).
- Evidence of **regular compliance reviews** (meeting minutes, review logs, action plans).
- Evidence that conveyancing staff know CQS exists and what it means (internal comms + training records).

---

## 2) Supervision and file review (this is usually heavily tested)
**What they check**
- Supervision is structured, documented and actually happens.
- File reviews occur at sensible points (not only at the end) and issues are escalated.

**Have ready**
- A written **supervision policy**: who supervises whom, ratios/coverage, escalation routes, holiday cover.
- A **file review framework** (stages, sample size, risk-based triggers).
- **Completed file review records** (recent examples): findings, remedial actions, learning fed back.
- A list of supervisors and evidence they are suitably experienced/trained (CQS also expects the *firm* to manage this; SRA sets the baseline supervision/competence expectations for individuals).

---

## 3) Conveyancing process aligned to the Law Society Conveyancing Protocol
**What they check**
- You have a documented end-to-end conveyancing process mapped to the Protocol.
- Staff use consistent templates/forms and current versions.

**Have ready**
- Your **process map / procedures** cross-referenced to Protocol stages (even a clear checklist is fine).
- Evidence of **standardised templates**: client care letters, reporting templates, completion statements, undertakings wording, etc.
- A **version control approach** for forms/standard conditions (how you ensure the “current” versions are used).
- Evidence of **exchange/completion controls**: authority to exchange, funds checks, completion/keys release process.

---

## 4) Client care, transparency and complaints handling
**What they check**
- Clients are given clear costs/service info at the outset and kept informed.
- Complaints are handled consistently, recorded, and you learn from them.

**Have ready**
- Client care procedure and **engagement terms** (including scope, costs estimates, likely disbursements, timescales).
- A communication/progress update standard (e.g., updates at milestones).
- Written **complaints policy** (including how clients are told about it).
- A **complaints register** with outcomes and timings.
- Evidence of **learning from complaints** (themes, actions taken, template updates, training topics).

---

## 5) Risk management: AML, sanctions, conflicts, undertakings, PII
**What they check**
- Controls are current, used in practice, and evidenced on files.
- Your risk approach reflects conveyancing fraud realities.

**Have ready**
- Firm-wide **AML policy** and matter risk assessment approach.
- **CDD/ID procedures** and examples of completed checks (including higher-risk triggers and EDD).
- **Source of funds/wealth** procedure and sample evidence from files.
- **Sanctions screening** procedure (who is screened, when, what tool, how results are recorded).
- **Conflicts checking** process (policy + evidence of checks).
- **Undertakings register** (open/closed status and chasing).
- Current **PII certificate** appropriate for conveyancing work.

---

## 6) Information security and fraud prevention (increasingly scrutinised)
**What they check**
- You actively manage cyber/funds diversion risk, not just have an IT policy on paper.
- Bank details verification is robust.

**Have ready**
- **Cyber/information security policy** and evidence staff know it.
- **Bank details verification procedure** (client and third party), including call-back verification and change-of-details controls.
- Secure communication approach (portal/encryption/controls for sending sensitive info).
- **Phishing/BEC awareness training records** (dates, attendees, content).
- **Business continuity plan** and evidence it’s been reviewed/tested.
- Data protection documentation (privacy policy; DPIAs if relevant).
- If you have it: **Cyber Essentials** (recommended, not always mandatory—but helps evidence good practice).

---

## 7) Training & competence (firm systems vs individual CPD)
**What they check**
- The firm has a training programme that supports Protocol compliance and risk controls.
- Mandatory CQS training is in date where required.

**Have ready**
- Record of **CQS mandatory training completion** (certificates, dates, who completed).
- Firm training matrix: induction, refresher training, role-based modules (AML, fraud, sanctions, complaints, Protocol).
- **Induction checklist** for new starters (including the firm’s conveyancing procedures and systems).
- CPD/learning records: CQS will expect **firm-wide records and planning**; **individual CPD/competence remains an SRA obligation**, so keep them aligned but don’t treat them as the same requirement.

---

## 8) File/case management and post-completion discipline
**What they check**
- You can demonstrate consistent file structure, diarising, and post-completion completion.
- Nothing relies on memory or one person.

**Have ready**
- Description of your case management system and standard file structure.
- Evidence key dates are diarised/monitored (exchange/completion/SDLT/registration/chase dates).
- Written **post-completion checklist** (SDLT submission, OS1/priority, AP1 submission, requisitions, lender discharge, serving notices where relevant).
- File retention policy and secure storage approach.
- Evidence of **regular file audits** and corrective actions.

---

# Practical “audit-ready” pack (what to assemble now)
If you want a single, assessor-friendly bundle, create a folder with:

1. **Index + org chart** (SRO, supervisors, complaints officer, MLRO/MLCO if applicable, IT lead)
2. All **policies/procedures** listed above (dated, version-controlled)
3. Last 6–12 months of:  
   - compliance review minutes/action logs  
   - file audit results + remediation  
   - complaints log + learning summary  
   - training matrix + completion evidence  
4. Registers: **complaints**, **undertakings**, (optionally) key risk incidents/near misses
5. Samples (anonymised if needed): a few completed **file review forms**, bank verification records, AML/SOF evidence, post-completion checklists

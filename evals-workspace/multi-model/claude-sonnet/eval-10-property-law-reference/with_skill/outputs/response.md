According to the Law Society, restrictive covenants are legal obligations that restrict how you can use your land, typically to protect neighboring properties. They "run with the land" meaning they bind future owners, not just the original parties who agreed to them.

**Key distinction for restrictive covenants:**

- **Pre-1926 covenants**: Governed by common law and equity. To be enforceable, you must show the covenant "touches and concerns" the land following the principle in *Tulk v Moxhay* (1848)
- **Post-1925 covenants**: Governed by Law of Property Act 1925, s.78 which provides for automatic annexation as established in *Federated Homes v Mill Lodge* (1980)

**HMLR Practice Guide 62** covers powers of attorney procedures that may be relevant if you need to modify or deal with covenant obligations on behalf of others.

**Modification or discharge of covenants:**
Under **s.84 Law of Property Act 1925**, you can apply to the **Upper Tribunal (Lands Chamber)** to modify or discharge restrictive covenants on grounds including:
- Obsolescence due to changed character of the neighborhood  
- No practical benefit to those entitled to the covenant
- The covenant impedes reasonable use of the land

**The Law Commission** has also published significant reports on restrictive covenant reform - their project "Making Land Work: Encumbrances" examines modernizing this area of law.

**Key sources to check:**
- Land Registry title documents (will show registered covenants)
- Original property deeds
- Local authority planning records
- Ordnance Survey maps for context

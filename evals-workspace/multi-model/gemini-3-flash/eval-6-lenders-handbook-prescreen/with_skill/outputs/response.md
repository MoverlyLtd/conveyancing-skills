### 1. Lease Eligibility (72 Years Remaining)
Under **Part 1, Section 5.14.1**, the handbook states that the unexpired lease term must satisfy the specific lender's minimum requirements set out in **Part 2**.

For a lease with **72 years remaining**, the eligibility for major lenders is as follows:

*   **Halifax:** **YES** — Their minimum is 70 years at completion.
*   **Nationwide:** **YES** — They are one of the most permissive major lenders, requiring only 55 years (or 30 years plus the mortgage term).
*   **Barclays:** **YES** — Their minimum is 70 years.
*   **Santander:** **YES** — Their minimum is 55 years.
*   **HSBC:** **YES** — Their minimum is 30 years plus the mortgage term (e.g., if the mortgage is 25 years, they require 55 years).

**Conclusion:** At 72 years, the property is currently eligible for all major Tier 1 lenders. However, it is approaching the 70-year threshold where some Tier 2 lenders may become restrictive.

### 2. Loft Conversion (Lack of Building Regulations)
Under **Part 1, Section 5.5.1**, you must take "all reasonable steps" to ensure the property has the necessary building regulation approvals.

#### The Enforcement Timeline
When assessing a breach, the "enforcement period" is critical. Under the Town and Country Planning Act 1990:
*   **The 4-year rule:** Local authorities are generally time-barred from taking enforcement action against building works (including loft conversions) 4 years after the work is substantially completed.
*   **The 10-year rule:** Applies to breaches of planning conditions or changes of use.

#### Reporting Decision (Section 5.5.2)
The Handbook states that if there is **no reasonable prospect of enforcement action**, you do not necessarily need to report this to the lender, provided you can still certify the title as **good and marketable**. 

However, if the work was completed recently (less than 4 years ago), you **must** report this to the lender. 

#### Resolution: Indemnity Insurance
If you cannot verify the completion date or if the lender requires further protection, **indemnity insurance** is the standard resolution. 
*   **Check Part 2 (Section 5.5.2):** Most lenders (like NatWest or Nationwide) will accept indemnity insurance in lieu of a building regulations completion certificate, provided the work is at least 12 months old and the policy is for the benefit of the lender and any subsequent owners.

### Summary Checklist for the Conveyancer
1.  **Verify the date of the loft conversion:** If it is older than 4 years, the risk of local authority enforcement is significantly reduced.
2.  **Confirm Title Quality:** Ensure the lack of sign-off doesn't affect the structural integrity (often identified via the valuation report). If the valuer identifies structural concerns, you **must** report this regardless of the age of the works (**HB 4.3**).
3.  **Arrange Indemnity:** If the client/lender remains concerned, obtain an quote for a "Lack of Building Regulations" indemnity policy.
4.  **Reporting:** If you cannot certify the title as "good and marketable" without the certificate, you must refer to the lender's **Part 2** and likely submit a formal report before exchange.

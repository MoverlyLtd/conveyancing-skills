According to the **Conveyancing Association (CA) Protocol (5th Edition)**, handling a 2021 rear extension without planning permission requires a specific procedural approach focused on enforcement timelines and risk mitigation.

### 1. Planning Permission Analysis (CA 4.0)
Since the extension was built in 2021, it falls within the critical enforcement windows. You must apply the following enforcement rules to determine the risk:

*   **The 4-Year Rule:** Applies to "operational development" (such as building an extension) or change of use to a single dwelling. If the work was completed **less than 4 years ago**, it is still subject to enforcement action.
*   **The 10-Year Rule:** Applies to breaches of planning conditions or other changes of use.
*   **The 12-Month Rule:** Applies specifically to listed building enforcement (which has no upper limit for prosecution in some contexts, but a 12-month window for certain types of stop notices).

**Protocol Action Plan for 2021 works:**
*   **If work completed >12 months but <4 years ago:** The seller should provide **indemnity insurance** to protect the buyer and lender against enforcement.
*   **If work completed <12 months ago:** The seller should obtain a **Regularisation Certificate** from the Local Authority or a bespoke indemnity policy.
*   **Action for all cases:** You must advise both the **client and the valuer**. The valuer must confirm the property is structurally sound despite the lack of formal records.

### 2. Building Regulations (A Separate Concern)
The CA Protocol (Section 4.0) mandates that **Building Regulations must be treated as a separate regime** from planning permission. Planning concerns land use; Building Regulations concern construction standards and safety.

For an extension built in 2021 (Post-1984, works >12 months but <10 years ago):
*   **Structural Works:** Because an extension is structural, the protocol dictates you should **recommend indemnity insurance at the seller's cost**.
*   **Surveyor Check:** You must advise the client and their surveyor that Building Regulations consent was not obtained. The surveyor should specifically confirm the structural integrity of the extension.
*   **Safety Warning:** Advise the client that a lack of building regulations may indicate underlying safety or compliance issues.

### 3. Communication and Delay Management
To comply with the protocol’s requirements for reducing transaction delays (CA 1.0–1.3), the following timeframes apply to resolving these enquiries:

*   **Response Time:** The seller’s lawyer should respond to enquiries regarding these missing permissions within **5 working days**.
*   **Acknowledgment:** Any correspondence regarding these defects should be acknowledged within **2 working days**.
*   **Updates:** If a substantive response (e.g., waiting for an indemnity quote) is not possible, provide **weekly updates** to all parties.
*   **Escalation:** If delays regarding these documents become unreasonable, the protocol suggests escalation: raise with the fee earner → then the senior partner/compliance officer → and finally refer to the relevant regulatory body (SRA or CLC) if necessary.

### 4. Seller’s Obligations (CA 1.7–1.8)
*   The seller is responsible for perfecting their own title defects.
*   A seller should not expect full market value if the title (or lack of permissions) is genuinely defective.
*   The seller’s conveyancer should have ideally identified this lack of permission during **Pre-Sale Preparation (CA 1.8)** and prepared the indemnity pack or evidence of completion dates before issuing the contract pack.

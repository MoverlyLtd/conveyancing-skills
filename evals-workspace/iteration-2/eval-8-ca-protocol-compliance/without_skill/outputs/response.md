# Protocol Guidance: Other Side Not Responding to Enquiries

## What the Protocol Says

The **Conveyancing Association (CA) Protocol (5th Edition)** and the **Law Society Conveyancing Protocol** both address the issue of timely responses to enquiries. Here's the key guidance:

### Response Timeframes

The CA Protocol sets clear expectations for responding to enquiries:

- **Initial pre-contract enquiries** should be raised and responded to promptly. The protocol expects responses within **10 working days** of receipt.
- **Additional/follow-up enquiries** should be kept to a minimum and only raised where genuinely necessary — but when raised, they should also be responded to within a reasonable timeframe (typically 10 working days).
- The protocol discourages unnecessary additional enquiries and encourages parties to limit enquiries to matters that are **relevant, reasonable, and proportionate**.

### Escalation Steps Under the Protocol

If the other side is not responding, the protocol envisages a structured escalation:

1. **Chase in writing** — Send a formal written chase (email is acceptable) requesting a response within a specified timeframe (e.g., 5 working days). Keep a record of all chasers sent.

2. **Escalate within the firm** — If the fee earner handling the matter is unresponsive, contact a supervisor or senior partner at the other firm directly.

3. **Notify your client** — Keep your client informed of the delays and the steps you are taking. Document this advice in your file.

4. **Consider regulatory complaints** — If the other side is persistently failing to respond and this constitutes a breach of professional obligations:
   - For **SRA-regulated firms**: solicitors have professional obligations to deal with matters in a timely manner and to respond to correspondence promptly.
   - For **CLC-regulated firms**: licensed conveyancers must act in clients' best interests and progress matters without unnecessary delay.

5. **Notice to Complete** — If contracts have already been exchanged and the delay is post-exchange, a formal Notice to Complete can be served (typically giving 10 working days to complete), though this is a contractual remedy rather than a protocol step.

### Reducing Unnecessary Enquiries

The protocol also addresses the root cause — it actively discourages excessive additional enquiries that cause delays:

- Enquiries should be **proportionate to the risk** in the transaction.
- Conveyancers should not raise enquiries on matters that are already addressed in the title documents, searches, or standard property information forms.
- The protocol encourages the use of **standard form enquiries** (CPSE, TA6/TA7/TA10) to streamline the process.

### Seller's Solicitor Obligations

The seller's solicitor has obligations under the protocol to:

- Provide a **comprehensive pre-contract package** upfront (including title, searches, property information forms, and standard replies to enquiries).
- Respond to enquiries **promptly and substantively** — not with holding replies or vague answers.
- Proactively update the buyer's solicitor on any changes in circumstances.

## Practical Steps You Should Take

1. **Document everything** — Keep a clear trail of when enquiries were sent and chasers issued.
2. **Set deadlines in writing** — Give the other side specific deadlines (e.g., "Please respond by [date], failing which we will escalate").
3. **Escalate progressively** — Fee earner → supervisor → managing partner → regulatory body.
4. **Report to your client** — Explain the delays, the impact on the transaction timeline, and the options available.
5. **Consider whether the delay is tactical** — If you suspect the seller is deliberately stalling, discuss with your client whether to set a firm deadline or consider withdrawing.

## Key Protocol Principle

The overarching principle is that **both parties should progress the transaction efficiently and without unnecessary delay**. A failure to respond to reasonable enquiries within a reasonable timeframe is a breach of the protocol's spirit and, potentially, of the conveyancer's professional obligations.

---

*Note: This guidance is based on general knowledge of the CA Protocol and Law Society Conveyancing Protocol principles. Always refer to the current edition of the relevant protocol for the precise obligations applicable to your transaction.*

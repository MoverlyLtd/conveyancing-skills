# CA Protocol Guidance: Other Side Not Responding to Enquiries

The Conveyancing Association Protocol (5th Edition, 2023) addresses this situation across several sections, with the core obligations falling under **Section 1: Reducing Transaction Delays** and **Section 2: Additional Enquiries**.

## What the Protocol Says

### 1. You Should Pick Up the Phone (CA 1.0)

The Protocol strongly encourages using **phone calls to resolve complex or stalled issues** rather than relying on protracted email exchanges. If enquiries have gone unanswered, a direct call to the other side's conveyancer is the Protocol-preferred first step.

### 2. Both Sides Have a Duty to Cooperate (CA 1.0)

The Protocol requires all conveyancers to:

- **Work cooperatively with the other side** and respond promptly to correspondence
- **Maintain regular updates** to the other lawyer and the estate agent
- Work on a **regular diarised file review cycle** so nothing falls through the cracks

The other side's failure to respond is itself a breach of CA Protocol obligations. If they are a CA Protocol member, they have committed to prompt and cooperative engagement.

### 3. Escalation via the Estate Agent (CA 1.0)

The Protocol contemplates the estate agent as a coordination point. You should:

- **Request full chain information** from the estate agent — this helps identify whether the delay is isolated or systemic through the chain
- Keep the estate agent updated on the lack of response — they often have commercial leverage to prompt action

### 4. Were the Enquiries Necessary? (CA 2.0, 2.4)

It's worth checking whether all your outstanding enquiries comply with Protocol expectations:

- Enquiries should be **limited to those relating to title or required by client/lender** — the seller's side may legitimately refuse "standard" enquiries that aren't specific to the property (CA 2.0)
- **Raise enquiries at the earliest possible stage** and implement supervisor review at regular intervals to catch issues early (CA 2.4)

If any of your enquiries are generic boilerplate rather than property-specific, the other side may be deprioritising them. Consider withdrawing non-essential enquiries to focus on what actually matters.

### 5. Seller's Conveyancer Obligations (CA 2.1–2.2)

The Protocol places specific obligations on the seller's conveyancer to:

- **Assist the buyer's lawyer** by advising the seller to provide required documents at the seller's expense — including things like s.106 agreements and adoption agreements (CA 2.1)
- **Review and augment the seller's replies** to ensure full information is given early (CA 2.2)

### 6. Staff Absence Cover (CA 1.2)

If the silence might be due to the fee earner being away, the Protocol requires firms to **ensure proper cover for staff absences** with monitoring of email and voicemail (CA 1.2). If you suspect absence is the cause, ask to speak to the covering fee earner.

## Practical Steps You Should Take

1. **Call the other side directly** — don't just send another email chaser (CA 1.0)
2. **Update the estate agent** and ask them to chase from their end (CA 1.0)
3. **Review your enquiries** — withdraw any that aren't genuinely necessary for this specific property or your client's lender (CA 2.0)
4. **Diarise a follow-up** — the Protocol expects regular file review, so set a specific chase date (CA 1.0)
5. **Update your client** — keep them informed about the delay and what you're doing about it
6. **Document everything** — if the matter falls through due to delay, your file should show you acted promptly and cooperatively throughout

## Key Point

The CA Protocol is clear: **both sides should work cooperatively and respond promptly**. The other side's silence is not consistent with their Protocol obligations. However, the Protocol also expects you to be pragmatic — use the phone, involve the estate agent, and make sure your own enquiries are proportionate and property-specific rather than generic boilerplate.

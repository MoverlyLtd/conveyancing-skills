# Restrictive Covenant Preventing Business Use — Legal Position & Guidance

## The Legal Position

A restrictive covenant prohibiting "business use" of a residential property is a common freehold covenant. Whether running a small home-based consultancy breaches it depends on several factors:

### 1. Construction of the Covenant Wording

The precise wording matters enormously. Common formulations include:

- **"Not to use the property other than as a private dwelling house"** — this is the most common and potentially the most restrictive. However, case law has established that incidental use of a home for work (where the predominant use remains residential) may not breach such a covenant.
- **"Not to carry on any trade or business on the property"** — narrower; focuses on active commercial activity rather than the character of occupation.
- **"Not to use for any purpose other than a single private residence"** — focuses on the nature of the occupation.

The key distinction courts draw is between **using the property as a business premises** versus **a resident who happens to do some work from home**. A sole consultant answering emails and taking calls from a home office, with no visiting clients, no signage, no employees, and no change to the residential character, is far less likely to be held in breach than someone converting a room into a client-facing office.

### 2. Relevant Case Law

- **Crest Nicholson Residential (South) Ltd v McAllister [2004] EWCA Civ 410** — important on construction of restrictive covenants and the extent to which they bind successors.
- **Re Bass Ltd's Application (1973)** — Lands Tribunal considered modification of a covenant restricting business use.
- **Coventry v Lawrence [2014] UKSC 13** — while primarily a nuisance case, the Supreme Court's discussion of the relationship between planning permission and private rights is relevant.
- The general principle from older authorities is that **"carrying on a business"** requires something more than merely working from home — it typically implies an activity that changes the character or use of the premises in a way noticeable to neighbours or the wider estate.

### 3. Practical Assessment

For your client's small consultancy, consider:

| Factor | Lower Risk | Higher Risk |
|--------|-----------|-------------|
| Client visits | None / rare | Regular foot traffic |
| Signage | None | Business sign displayed |
| Employees | None on-site | Staff attending daily |
| Deliveries | Normal residential | Commercial volumes |
| Character of property | Unchanged | Visible commercial adaptation |
| Hours | Normal | Extended / antisocial |

A consultant working from a home office with no external indicators of commercial activity is at the **lower-risk end** of the spectrum, but this is not risk-free — particularly if the covenant is actively enforced by a management company or neighbouring freeholder.

### 4. Who Can Enforce?

- The **original covenantee** (typically the developer/vendor) and their successors in title to the benefited land.
- **Neighbouring owners** on the same estate if the covenant was imposed under a building scheme (scheme of development), giving mutual enforceability.
- A **management company** if one exists with the benefit of the covenants.

The practical likelihood of enforcement matters. On a modern estate with an active management company, enforcement is more probable than on older titles where the benefited land is unclear.

### 5. Options if the Covenant is Potentially Breached

1. **Seek consent** — if the covenant allows application to a named party for consent (common on estate developments), apply for a waiver or licence.
2. **Restrictive covenant indemnity insurance** — available where the covenant has not been openly breached and no complaints received. Relatively inexpensive. Must be obtained *before* any breach or disclosure to the beneficiary.
3. **Application to the Upper Tribunal (Lands Chamber)** under **section 84 of the Law of Property Act 1925** to have the covenant **modified or discharged** on grounds including:
   - Changes in the character of the neighbourhood
   - The covenant being obsolete
   - No practical benefit of substantial value to the person entitled to enforce it
   - It being contrary to the public interest
4. **Negotiate a release** with the person(s) entitled to the benefit — often practical on older titles.

## Authoritative Guidance Sources

### Primary Legislation
- **Law of Property Act 1925, ss. 56, 78, 79, 84** — creation, annexation, and modification/discharge of restrictive covenants
- **Town and Country Planning Act 1990, s. 106** (if planning-related)

### Key Practitioner Texts
- **Megarry & Wade: The Law of Real Property** — Chapter 32 (Freehold Covenants) is the definitive academic treatment
- **Preston & Newsom: Restrictive Covenants Affecting Freehold Land** — the specialist practitioner text
- **Emmet & Farrand on Title** — practical conveyancing guidance on covenants
- **Wolstenholme & Cherry's Conveyancing Statutes** — annotated statutory provisions

### Professional Guidance
- **The Law Society Practice Notes** on restrictive covenants
- **Land Registry Practice Guide 19** — guidance on notices and restrictions (relevant to covenant protection on the register)

### Upper Tribunal (Lands Chamber)
- The Tribunal publishes decisions on s.84 applications at [decisions.tribunals.gov.uk](https://decisions.tribunals.gov.uk) — search for modification/discharge applications involving business-use covenants for analogous cases.

### Planning Distinction
- **Planning permission does not override a restrictive covenant.** Even if your client has (or could obtain) planning consent for home working or mixed use, the private law restriction remains enforceable. These are separate legal regimes (*Wheeler v JJ Saunders Ltd [1996]*).

## Practical Advice for the Client

1. **Obtain the exact covenant wording** from the title register and any referred-to transfer/conveyance.
2. **Identify the beneficiary** — who can actually enforce? Check the charges register and original estate documentation.
3. **Assess the practical risk** — has the covenant been enforced before? Are there other home businesses on the estate?
4. **If low-risk and no prior breach**: consider restrictive covenant indemnity insurance as a pragmatic solution.
5. **If higher-risk or the client wants certainty**: seek consent from the beneficiary, or consider a s.84 application.
6. **Do not disclose the intended use to the covenant beneficiary** before obtaining insurance, as this will typically invalidate the ability to insure.

---

*This guidance is based on the law of England and Wales. Restrictive covenant law differs in Scotland (where real burdens under the Title Conditions (Scotland) Act 2003 apply) and Northern Ireland.*

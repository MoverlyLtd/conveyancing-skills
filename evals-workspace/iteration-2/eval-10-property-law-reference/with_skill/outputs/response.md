# Restrictive Covenant Preventing Business Use — Legal Position and Authoritative Guidance

## Overview

A restrictive covenant preventing business use of a residential property is a legally binding restriction that runs with the land. Running a small consultancy from home — even if low-impact — is capable of constituting a breach if the covenant's wording catches it. The legal position depends critically on the **exact wording** of the covenant, who has the **benefit** of it, and whether the proposed use genuinely falls within the restriction's scope.

## The Legal Position

### 1. Interpretation of the Covenant Wording

The starting point is always the precise language of the covenant. Common formulations include:

- *"Not to use the property otherwise than as a private dwelling house"*
- *"Not to carry on any trade or business on the property"*
- *"Not to use for any purpose other than residential"*

Each formulation has a different scope. A covenant restricting use to a "private dwelling house" may be breached by any identifiable business activity, whereas a prohibition on "trade or business" requires consideration of whether a home-based consultancy (with no visiting clients, signage, or employees) genuinely constitutes "business use" of the property as opposed to incidental use of a home office.

According to the Upper Tribunal (Lands Chamber) guidance, "restrictive covenants are often imposed when someone sells their land, for example to prevent the buyer from using it for business, to prevent building on it, or to ensure that they keep to a particular style or size of building." The obligation to comply remains regardless of how often the land changes hands or how outdated the covenants may appear. (Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/upper-tribunal-lands-chamber-restrictive-covenants-t608-and-t617/guide-for-applications-to-discharge-or-modify-restrictive-covenants-t608)

### 2. Who Can Enforce the Covenant?

A restrictive covenant is only enforceable by a person who holds the **benefit** of it. According to the GOV.UK guidance, "restrictive covenants are imposed by deed, often on a sale of land, but they are private arrangements – only the person who imposed the covenant, and anyone who owns any of that person's land later, can enforce the restrictive covenant." (Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/upper-tribunal-lands-chamber-restrictive-covenants-t608-and-t617/guide-for-applications-to-discharge-or-modify-restrictive-covenants-t608)

If the original covenantee's land has been extensively developed or subdivided, it may be difficult to identify who (if anyone) retains the benefit and standing to enforce. However, this should not be assumed without investigation — enforceability is a question of law that depends on whether the benefit was annexed to land, assigned, or is part of a building scheme.

### 3. Options Available to Your Client

#### Option A: Argue the Use Is Not a Breach

If the consultancy involves purely desk-based work from home with no visiting clients, no employees, no signage, and no material change to the residential character of the property, there may be an argument that this does not constitute "business use" within the meaning of the covenant. This is a question of construction of the specific wording.

#### Option B: Seek Consent from the Beneficiary

If the person(s) entitled to the benefit of the covenant can be identified, your client could approach them to seek express consent or a formal release/modification of the covenant.

#### Option C: Apply to the Upper Tribunal for Discharge or Modification

According to section 84 of the Law of Property Act 1925, the Upper Tribunal (Lands Chamber) has power to discharge or modify restrictive covenants on the following grounds:

- **(a) Obsolescence** — that by reason of changes in the character of the property or neighbourhood, the restriction ought to be deemed obsolete;
- **(aa) Reasonable user** — that the continued existence of the restriction would impede some reasonable use of the land, provided it does not secure practical benefits of substantial value to those entitled, or is contrary to the public interest, and money would be adequate compensation;
- **(b) Agreement** — that persons entitled to the benefit have agreed (expressly or by implication) to discharge or modification;
- **(c) No injury** — that the proposed discharge or modification will not injure those entitled to the benefit.

(Source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/Geo5/15-16/20/section/84)

The application is made using Form T379. The GOV.UK guidance notes that "the law on restrictions affecting land is complex and it is advisable to seek legal advice before applying to the tribunal." (Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/upper-tribunal-lands-chamber-restrictive-covenants-t608-and-t617/guide-for-applications-to-discharge-or-modify-restrictive-covenants-t608)

#### Option D: Restrictive Covenant Indemnity Insurance

Where the risk of enforcement is low (e.g., the beneficiary cannot be identified, or the covenant is historic and has never been enforced), it may be possible to obtain restrictive covenant indemnity insurance. This does not make the breach lawful, but protects against the financial consequences of enforcement action. **Important:** approaching the beneficiary or drawing attention to the breach before obtaining insurance may void the policy. Insurance should be arranged through a conveyancer before any steps are taken that could alert the beneficiary.

### 4. Planning Permission Is Separate

According to the Upper Tribunal guidance, "restrictive covenants have nothing to do with the town and country planning system – a restrictive covenant can prevent development even if it has planning permission." (Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/upper-tribunal-lands-chamber-restrictive-covenants-t608-and-t617/guide-for-applications-to-discharge-or-modify-restrictive-covenants-t608)

Even if the consultancy use would not require planning permission (e.g., it falls within permitted use of a dwelling), this does not override the restrictive covenant.

## Important Disclaimer

This information is provided for reference purposes only and does not constitute legal advice. The legal position on restrictive covenants is fact-specific and depends on the precise wording of the covenant, the identity and standing of potential beneficiaries, and the nature of the proposed use. **Your client should discuss this with their conveyancer or a property litigation specialist before taking any action**, including commencing the consultancy, approaching any beneficiary, or applying for indemnity insurance.

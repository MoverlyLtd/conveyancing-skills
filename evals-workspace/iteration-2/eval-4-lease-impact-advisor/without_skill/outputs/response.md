# Lease Length Advice — 68-Year Lease at £425,000

## Summary

A 68-year lease is a significant concern and will materially affect the saleability and value of this flat. Here's what you need to know as the listing agent.

---

## 1. The 80-Year Threshold

The critical marker in leasehold law is **80 years remaining**. Once a lease drops below 80 years:

- **Marriage value** becomes payable on any lease extension. This means the freeholder is entitled to a share (typically 50%) of the increase in the property's value that a lease extension would create.
- The cost of extending the lease increases significantly — and accelerates the shorter it gets.

At **68 years**, this flat is already 12 years past that threshold, meaning a lease extension will be noticeably more expensive than it would have been at 81+ years.

## 2. Impact on Value and Marketability

- **Mortgage difficulties:** Many lenders require a minimum unexpired lease term — commonly 70–85 years at the *end* of the mortgage term (not today). A 68-year lease means a 25-year mortgage would leave only 43 years remaining at maturity. Most mainstream lenders will **decline** to lend on this basis, severely restricting the buyer pool to cash purchasers or specialist lenders.
- **Reduced value:** Properties with short leases trade at a discount. Industry estimates suggest a 68-year lease could reduce the value by **10–20%+** compared to a long lease (say 125+ years). The exact discount depends on ground rent levels, the freeholder's attitude, and local market conditions.
- **Buyer caution:** Informed buyers and their solicitors will flag this immediately. It often leads to protracted negotiations or collapsed sales.

## 3. Lease Extension Costs

Under the **Leasehold Reform, Housing and Urban Development Act 1993**, a qualifying leaseholder (who has owned for 2+ years) has a statutory right to extend by 90 years at a peppercorn (zero) ground rent.

The premium for extending a 68-year lease on a £425,000 flat will depend on:
- The current ground rent and any escalation clauses
- The capitalisation rate and deferment rate used in the valuation
- The marriage value split (50/50 by statute)

A rough estimate for the extension premium might be in the range of **£20,000–£40,000+**, but this varies significantly. A specialist lease extension surveyor (RICS-qualified) should be instructed for a proper valuation.

## 4. The Leasehold Reform (Ground Rent) Act 2022 and Upcoming Reforms

- The **Leasehold and Freehold Reform Act 2024** received Royal Assent and, when fully commenced, is expected to:
  - **Abolish marriage value** — this would substantially reduce extension costs for sub-80-year leases
  - Standardise the valuation methodology
  - Make the process simpler and cheaper
- However, as of early 2026, key provisions (including the marriage value abolition) have **not yet been commenced**. The timeline remains uncertain, so you cannot rely on these reforms being in force for a current transaction.

## 5. Practical Advice for Listing

1. **Be transparent.** The lease length must be disclosed. Material Information regulations (National Trading Standards) require lease details in property listings.
2. **Consider advising the seller to extend first.** If the seller has owned for 2+ years and can afford the premium, extending the lease before marketing will significantly improve saleability and likely achieve a higher net sale price even after the extension cost.
3. **Alternatively, price accordingly.** If selling with the short lease, the asking price should reflect the cost a buyer will incur to extend. At £425,000, buyers will mentally deduct £25,000–£40,000+ for the extension, plus factor in risk and hassle.
4. **Highlight if extension is in progress.** If the seller has already served a Section 42 notice (formal notice of claim to extend), this is a positive — the benefit of that notice can be assigned to the buyer on completion.
5. **Check the ground rent.** If the ground rent is high or has doubling/escalating clauses, this compounds the problem. Some lenders treat escalating ground rents as an additional reason to decline lending.

## 6. Key Risks to Flag

| Risk | Detail |
|------|--------|
| **Mortgage refusal** | Most high-street lenders will not lend on a 68-year lease for standard mortgage terms |
| **Increasing cost of delay** | Every year that passes makes the extension more expensive (the lease is a wasting asset) |
| **Difficult freeholder** | If the freeholder is unresponsive or adversarial, the statutory process can take 6–12+ months |
| **Collapsed sales** | Buyers' solicitors routinely advise against short leases; sales fall through frequently |

## 7. Bottom Line

A 68-year lease is firmly in "short lease" territory. It will limit your buyer pool, reduce the achievable price, and likely extend the time to sell. The strongest advice you can give your seller client is to **explore extending the lease before or concurrently with marketing**. If they choose not to, price the property to reflect the reality and be upfront with prospective buyers about the lease position.

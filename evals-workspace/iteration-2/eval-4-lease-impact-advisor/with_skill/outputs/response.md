# Lease Impact Assessment: 68 Years Remaining — £425,000 Flat

## The headline: this is a problem lease

At 68 years remaining, your listing falls into the **CRITICAL** risk band. This will significantly affect who can buy the property, how long it takes to sell, and the price you'll achieve. Here's what you need to know.

---

## 🔴 Saleability Impact

With 68 years on the lease, you should expect:

- **Severely restricted buyer pool** — most buyers need a mortgage, and most mainstream lenders won't touch this
- **Price discount of 15–30%** compared to an equivalent property with a long lease — potentially **£63,750 to £127,500** off what you might otherwise achieve
- **Much longer time on market** — fewer eligible buyers means fewer viewings, fewer offers, longer chains
- **Cash buyer territory** — realistically, you're mainly appealing to cash buyers or those using niche/specialist lenders

This isn't a "flag it in the particulars and hope for the best" situation. It will dominate the transaction.

## 💰 Marriage Value — The Hidden Cost

Because the lease is **under 80 years**, something called **marriage value** applies. This is a legal concept that significantly increases the cost of extending the lease.

When a lease is extended, the property increases in value (because a long lease is worth more). Under current law, the leaseholder must pay the freeholder **50% of that increase** on top of the normal premium.

This can add **tens of thousands of pounds** to the extension cost.

> **Note:** The Leasehold and Freehold Reform Act 2024 abolishes marriage value, but this provision has **not yet come into force**. Until it does, marriage value applies. Your vendor's conveyancer should advise on the current position.

## 📋 What You Should Recommend to Your Vendor

### Option 1: Extend Before Marketing (Strongly Recommended)

The vendor should seriously consider extending the lease **before putting the property on the market**. This would:

- Move the lease from CRITICAL into GREEN territory (68 + 90 = 158 years)
- Open up the **full buyer pool** — every lender, every buyer type
- Remove the 15–30% price discount
- Dramatically reduce time on market

**Estimated extension cost: £42,500 – £85,000** (premium to freeholder, including marriage value), plus **£2,000 – £4,000** in professional fees (solicitor and surveyor).

That's a significant outlay, but compare it to the **£63,750 – £127,500** potential price discount from marketing with a short lease. The numbers strongly favour extending first.

**Routes to extend:**

- **Statutory route (Section 42 notice):** Requires the vendor to have owned for at least 2 years. Adds 90 years, ground rent drops to zero. The freeholder cannot refuse. Takes 6–12 months typically.
- **Informal negotiation:** No ownership requirement. Terms are negotiable but the freeholder sets the price. Can be faster.
- **Assign the benefit:** The vendor starts the statutory process, then assigns the right to the buyer on completion. The buyer completes the extension. This is a good option if the vendor hasn't owned for 2 years, or if the buyer wants to control the process.

### Option 2: Market As-Is with Full Disclosure

If the vendor won't extend, you need to:

- **Price realistically** — factor in the 15–30% discount from day one
- **Target your marketing** — cash buyers, investors, portfolio landlords
- **Be upfront in particulars** — state the lease length clearly; surprises kill deals
- **Prepare for longer timescales** — this will not be a quick sale
- **Brief the vendor** — they need to understand why offers will be lower than comparable long-lease properties

# CA Protocol: Handling a Rear Extension Built in 2021 Without Planning Permission Records

## Summary

The Conveyancing Association (CA) Protocol (5th Edition) addresses situations where building works have been carried out without clear planning permission records. The Protocol aims to reduce unnecessary additional enquiries and delays by setting out a pragmatic framework for how such issues should be handled between buyer's and seller's conveyancers.

## Key CA Protocol Provisions

### 1. Seller's Obligations Under the Protocol

The seller's conveyancer should:

- **Provide upfront information**: The Protocol requires the seller's side to supply comprehensive property information at the outset, including details of any alterations, extensions, or building works carried out during the seller's ownership.
- **Disclose in the Property Information Form (TA6)**: The seller should disclose the rear extension in the TA6 form, noting that planning permission records are not available.
- **Supply any available documentation**: Any building control completion certificates, architect's drawings, builder's invoices, or correspondence with the local authority should be provided proactively.

### 2. Buyer's Conveyancer — Limiting Additional Enquiries

The CA Protocol discourages excessive additional enquiries. Specifically:

- **Avoid unnecessary or speculative enquiries**: The buyer's conveyancer should not raise enquiries that could be resolved through their own searches or publicly available information (e.g., checking the local authority planning portal).
- **Check the planning register first**: Before raising enquiries with the seller, the buyer's conveyancer should check whether the extension may have been built under **Permitted Development (PD) rights**, which would not require a formal planning application. A single-storey rear extension built in 2021 may well fall within PD limits depending on its size and the property type.
- **Raise focused, specific enquiries**: Where genuine issues remain after searches, the buyer's conveyancer should raise targeted enquiries rather than broad, open-ended ones.

### 3. Practical Approach to the Missing Planning Permission

Under the CA Protocol framework, the following steps would typically apply:

#### a) Determine if Planning Permission Was Actually Required
- A rear extension may fall within **Permitted Development** rights (Part 1, Class A of the GPDO 2015), meaning no planning application was needed.
- The buyer's conveyancer should check the local authority planning portal and consider whether PD rights apply based on the extension's dimensions, position, and the property type.

#### b) Building Regulations Compliance
- Even if planning permission was not required, **Building Regulations approval** almost certainly was for a 2021 extension.
- The Protocol would expect the seller to provide a **Building Regulations Completion Certificate** or, if unavailable, explain its absence.
- If no completion certificate exists, the buyer's conveyancer may need to consider whether a **regularisation certificate** can be obtained from the local authority.

#### c) Indemnity Insurance
- The CA Protocol recognises that indemnity insurance may be an appropriate and proportionate solution where planning or building regulations documentation is incomplete.
- For a 2021 extension (only ~5 years old at the point of transaction), obtaining a **retrospective regularisation** from Building Control may be preferable to indemnity insurance, since the local authority could still inspect and certify the works.
- However, if the lender accepts indemnity insurance and the risk is low, this may be the most pragmatic route.

#### d) Lender Requirements
- The buyer's conveyancer must consider the **UK Finance Lender's Handbook (Part 1)** requirements regarding unauthorised alterations.
- Most lenders require either evidence of consent/approval or appropriate indemnity insurance for works carried out without the necessary permissions.

### 4. Protocol Emphasis on Proportionality

The CA Protocol's overarching principle is **proportionality**:

- The issue should not be allowed to cause disproportionate delay to the transaction.
- Where the extension is clearly within PD limits, a simple confirmation or certificate of lawful development may suffice.
- Where Building Regulations compliance is uncertain, a focused approach (regularisation or insurance) should be agreed promptly.
- The Protocol discourages the buyer's conveyancer from treating missing paperwork as a reason to raise extensive rounds of additional enquiries when practical solutions exist.

## Conclusion

The CA Protocol would expect this issue to be handled pragmatically: the seller discloses the extension upfront with whatever documentation is available; the buyer's conveyancer checks whether planning permission was actually required (PD rights); Building Regulations compliance is verified or addressed through regularisation or indemnity insurance; and the matter is resolved without unnecessary delay or disproportionate additional enquiries. The Protocol's core ethos is that known, common issues like this should be dealt with through established, proportionate solutions rather than becoming a source of protracted correspondence.

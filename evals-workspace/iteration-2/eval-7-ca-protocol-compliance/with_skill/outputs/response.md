# CA Protocol Compliance: Rear Extension Built 2021 Without Planning Permission Records

## Summary

A rear extension built in 2021 without planning permission records triggers two separate CA Protocol Section 4.0 decision trees — one for **planning permission** and one for **building regulations**. Both require active steps given the recency of the works.

---

## 1. Planning Permission Analysis (CA Protocol 4.0)

Following the CA Protocol decision tree for planning permission:

**Has the work required planning permission?**

First, establish whether the extension actually needed planning permission. Many single-storey rear extensions fall within **permitted development rights** (PD) and don't require express planning permission. The absence of planning permission *records* doesn't necessarily mean permission was needed but not obtained — it may mean PD applied.

**If PD applies:** No planning permission was required, so absence of records is expected. Consider obtaining a **Lawful Development Certificate (LDC)** from the local authority to confirm PD status — this provides certainty for the buyer and lender.

**If planning permission was required but not obtained:**

The extension was built in 2021, meaning it was completed approximately 4–5 years ago (as of 2026). Following the decision tree:

- Work completed **>4 years ago** without concealment (and this is not a change of use) → **No further enforcement action** can be taken by the local authority, as the 4-year enforcement period has expired.
- However, if the works were completed **less than 4 years ago** at the date of the transaction → The seller should obtain **indemnity insurance** (if >12 months but <4 years) or a **regularisation certificate/bespoke indemnity** (if <12 months).

**Critical step in ALL cases without permission:** The CA Protocol requires that:
- The **client must be advised** that planning permission was not obtained
- The **valuer/surveyor must be advised** and should confirm the extension is structurally sound
- The valuer should be given the opportunity to reassess their valuation in light of the planning position

**Note on timing:** The exact completion date in 2021 matters. If completed in early 2021, the 4-year enforcement window has likely passed by 2026. If late 2021, it may be marginal — check the precise date carefully.

## 2. Building Regulations Analysis (CA Protocol 4.0)

A rear extension is almost certainly structural work requiring building regulations approval. Following the building regulations decision tree:

**Works are post-1984, and no completion certificate has been revealed:**

Since the works were carried out in 2021 (approximately 4–5 years ago), this falls into the category of **>12 months but <10 years without a certificate**:

- For **structural works** (which a rear extension is): The CA Protocol recommends:
  1. **Advise the client and lender** that building regulations approval was not obtained
  2. **Check with the surveyor** — the surveyor should confirm the extension is structurally sound
  3. **Recommend indemnity insurance at the seller's cost**

Additionally, in **all cases** without a building regulations certificate, the CA Protocol requires:
- Advise the client **and** surveyor that local authority consent was not obtained
- The surveyor should confirm the works are structurally sound
- Advise the client that the lack of building regulations compliance may indicate **safety compliance issues** (fire safety, structural integrity, electrical safety, drainage etc.)

## 3. Practical Steps for the Conveyancer

Based on the CA Protocol guidance, the following steps should be taken:

### Immediate Actions
1. **Establish whether PD rights applied** — check the property type, extension size, and any Article 4 directions. Consider obtaining an LDC if PD is arguable.
2. **Request building regulations records** from the local authority search (if not already done) — a completion certificate may exist even if the seller hasn't provided it.
3. **Raise enquiries with the seller's conveyancer** asking for any evidence of planning permission, building regulations approval, or building control sign-off.

### If No Approvals Are Found
4. **Advise the buyer** about the absence of both planning permission and building regulations approval, and the implications of each (ref: CA 4.0).
5. **Notify the surveyor/valuer** so they can inspect the extension and confirm it is structurally sound and factor the planning/building regs position into their valuation (ref: CA 4.0).
6. **Report to the lender** — the lender needs to know about the absence of building regulations approval. Check the UK Finance Lender's Handbook Part 1 and the specific lender's Part 2 requirements before referring (ref: CA 5.0).
7. **Obtain indemnity insurance** at the seller's cost for the building regulations position. For planning, if the 4-year enforcement period has passed, indemnity may not be necessary but should still be considered (ref: CA 4.0).

### Enquiries to Limit (CA Protocol 2.0)
The CA Protocol reminds conveyancers to limit additional enquiries to those relating to title or required by the client/lender. However, planning and building regulations for a 2021 extension are clearly title-related and necessary — these are not speculative "standard" enquiries.

## 5. Important Caveats

- The CA Protocol states each transaction must be judged on its merits — the above is general guidance, not a substitute for professional judgment on the specific facts.
- Where the CA Protocol conflicts with regulatory requirements (SRA/CLC) or Law Society guidance, the latter prevails.
- The 4-year planning enforcement period applies to building operations; if there is any element of **change of use**, the period is **10 years** instead.
- Concealment of works can extend enforcement periods under the Planning and Compensation Act 1991 provisions — ensure there was no deliberate concealment.

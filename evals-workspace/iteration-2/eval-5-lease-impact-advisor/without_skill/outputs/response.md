# Lease Length Assessment: 95-Year Leasehold Flat

## Short Answer

A 95-year lease is **not an immediate crisis**, but it does warrant attention and your buyer should be made aware of the implications. It sits in a zone where action within the next few years is advisable rather than urgent.

## Key Considerations

### 1. The 80-Year Threshold

The critical milestone for leasehold properties is **80 years remaining**. Once a lease drops below 80 years:

- **Marriage value** becomes payable on lease extensions — the freeholder is entitled to 50% of the "marriage value" (the increase in property value resulting from the extension). This makes extensions significantly more expensive.
- At 95 years, your buyer has roughly **15 years before hitting this threshold**, which provides a reasonable but not unlimited window.

### 2. Mortgage Implications

- Most mortgage lenders require a minimum unexpired lease term, typically **70–85 years at the end of the mortgage term** (not just at the point of purchase).
- For a 25-year mortgage on a 95-year lease, the remaining term at the end of the mortgage would be **70 years** — this is at or below many lenders' minimum thresholds.
- **Check the specific lender's requirements** in Part 2 of the UK Finance Lenders' Handbook. Some lenders may decline or impose conditions.

### 3. Cost of Extension Now vs Later

- The premium for extending a 95-year lease will be **materially cheaper** than extending an 85-year or 80-year lease.
- The buyer should factor in the likely cost of a lease extension when negotiating the purchase price or deciding whether to proceed.
- Under the current statutory regime (Leasehold Reform, Housing and Urban Development Act 1993), a qualifying tenant can extend by **90 years** at a peppercorn rent after owning for **2 years**.

### 4. Leasehold Reform

- The **Leasehold and Freehold Reform Act 2024** received Royal Assent and includes provisions to:
  - Remove the 2-year ownership requirement for statutory extensions
  - Remove marriage value from premium calculations
  - Potentially allow 990-year extensions
- However, **key provisions are not yet in force** as of early 2026, and the commencement timeline remains uncertain. Do not advise the buyer to rely on reforms that haven't been commenced.

### 5. Valuation & Resale Impact

- Properties with leases under 100 years can start to see a **diminishing value** compared to longer leases.
- Future buyers may face the same mortgage concerns, potentially narrowing the resale market.
- A short(ish) lease is a **negotiating point** — the buyer may be able to negotiate a reduced purchase price to reflect the cost of a future extension.

## Practical Advice

1. **Check the lender's handbook requirements** — confirm the specific lender will lend on a 95-year lease for the proposed mortgage term.
2. **Obtain a lease extension cost estimate** — instruct a specialist surveyor to estimate the premium so the buyer understands the financial commitment.
3. **Consider negotiating with the seller** — the seller could extend the lease before completion (informal or statutory route), or the price could be adjusted to reflect extension costs.
4. **Advise the buyer in writing** about the diminishing lease, the 80-year cliff edge, mortgage implications, and the importance of extending sooner rather than later.
5. **Report to the lender** if their handbook requires notification of leases below a certain threshold — many specify reporting at 80 or 85 years, but some set the bar higher.
6. **Do not rely on upcoming leasehold reform** — advise based on the law as it stands.

## Summary

At 95 years, the lease is **manageable but declining**. The buyer is not in crisis territory, but they should plan to extend the lease within the next few years — ideally soon after the 2-year qualifying period (under current law) — to avoid the significant cost increase that kicks in below 80 years. Ensure the mortgage lender is content, advise the buyer clearly, and factor extension costs into the transaction economics.

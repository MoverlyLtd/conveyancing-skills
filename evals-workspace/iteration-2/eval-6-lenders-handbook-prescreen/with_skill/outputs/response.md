# Lender's Handbook Pre-Screen: Leasehold Property with Short Lease & Unauthorised Loft Conversion

Two issues have been identified that trigger handbook requirements. Both require action before you can provide an unqualified Certificate of Title.

---

## Issue 1: Lease Term — 72 Years Remaining

**Triggered check:** 9.1 — Does the unexpired lease term meet the lender's minimum? *(HB 5.14.1)*

### What the Handbook Says

The unexpired lease term must meet the **lender's specific minimum requirement**, which is a Part 2 matter. Each lender sets its own minimum — typically expressed either as:

- A **minimum remaining term at completion** (e.g. 55, 70, 80, or 85 years), or
- A **minimum term beyond the end of the mortgage** (e.g. 30–50 years beyond mortgage expiry)

### Assessment for 72 Years

At 72 years unexpired, you are in a **risk zone for many mainstream lenders**. For example:

- Some lenders require a minimum of **70 years** remaining at completion — 72 years would scrape through but only just
- Others require **80 or 85 years** minimum — 72 years would **fail** these requirements
- Some require the lease to extend a set number of years **beyond the mortgage term** — e.g. if the mortgage is 25 years and the lender requires 40 years beyond expiry, you'd need 65 years minimum (72 would pass), but if they require 50 years beyond, you'd need 75 years (72 would **fail**)

### Required Action

1. **Check Part 2** for the borrower's specific lender — read their minimum lease term requirement at section 5.14.1 in the lender's Part 2 reference file
2. If 72 years **fails** the lender's minimum:
   - **Report to the lender before exchange** (HB 2.3)
   - Consider whether a **lease extension** can be negotiated/obtained before completion
   - Note that sub-80-year leases attract **increasingly steep marriage value** under the current statutory regime, which may affect value and lender appetite
3. Even if 72 years technically meets the minimum, flag it as a matter that may affect **future marketability and value** — the valuer's assumptions about the lease term should be checked (HB 4.3, check 3.2)

### Additional Leasehold Checks Triggered

Since this is leasehold, you must also satisfy yourself on all other section 9 checks, including:

| Check | Requirement |
|-------|-------------|
| 9.2 | No forfeiture on insolvency clause *(HB 5.14.2)* |
| 9.3 | No unreasonable restriction on mortgage/assignment *(HB 5.14.3)* |
| 9.4–9.5 | Adequate rights of access, support, shelter + maintenance covenants *(HB 5.14.4–5.14.6)* |
| 9.6 | Other flats' leases in substantially similar form *(HB 5.14.7)* |
| 9.7 | Ground rent — is it fixed/readily ascertainable/reasonable? Report if increases may materially affect value *(HB 5.14.9)* |
| 9.8 | Any significant service charge increase foreseen? *(HB 5.14.10)* |
| 9.10 | Clear ground rent and service charge receipts on completion *(HB 5.14.12)* |
| 9.11 | Notice of mortgage to landlord and management company post-completion *(HB 5.14.13)* |
| 9.14 | If a relevant building under the Building Safety Act 2022 (5+ storeys): request remediation confirmation and Landlord's Certificates *(HB 5.14.17)* |

## Issue 2: Loft Conversion Without Building Regulations Sign-Off

**Triggered check:** 13.6 — Does the property have necessary building regulation approval? *(HB 5.5.1)*
**Also triggered:** 13.7 — Any breach of building conditions? *(HB 5.5.1–5.5.2)*

### What the Handbook Says

You must take **all reasonable steps** to ensure the property has all necessary planning consents and building regulation approval *(HB 5.5.1)*. A loft conversion is notifiable building work under the Building Regulations 2010 and requires either:

- A **completion certificate** from the local authority (Building Control), or
- A **final certificate** from an Approved Inspector

Without either, this is a **breach of building regulations**.

### Assessment

A loft conversion without building regulations sign-off means:

1. **The work may not comply with structural, fire safety, insulation, or staircase requirements** — this is a material concern for any lender
2. The lender's **valuation may have assumed the work was approved** — check 3.2 requires you to verify the valuer's assumptions are correct *(HB 4.3)*
3. There may be a **discrepancy between the valuation description and the actual legal position** — check 3.1 *(HB 4.2)*

### Reporting Decision Tree

```
Can you certify title as good and marketable?
├── Has 12+ months elapsed since the work was completed?
│   ├── YES → Local authority enforcement powers have expired
│   │         (s.36 Building Act 1984 — 12-month limitation)
│   │   └── Can you certify good/marketable title despite the breach?
│   │       ├── YES → Arrange indemnity insurance for building regs
│   │       │         breach → proceed (if Part 2 accepts indemnity)
│   │       └── NO → Report to lender (HB 2.3)
│   └── NO → Local authority can still enforce (injunction/removal)
│       └── Report to lender — cannot certify good title
└── In all cases: check valuer's assumptions are correct (HB 4.3)
```

### Required Action

1. **Establish when the loft conversion was carried out**
   - If **more than 12 months ago**: the local authority's power to enforce under s.36 Building Act 1984 has expired (they cannot require removal or alteration). However, the work may still be non-compliant
   - If **less than 12 months ago**: enforcement action remains possible — this is a serious title issue

2. **If time-barred (>12 months):**
   - Obtain **indemnity insurance** covering the building regulations breach — the policy must protect the lender (check 22.1, HB 9) and be on terms acceptable to the lender (check 22.2 — check Part 2)
   - Satisfy yourself that you can provide an unqualified certificate of title with the indemnity in place
   - Consider whether a **retrospective building regulations application** (regularisation certificate) might be more appropriate — some lenders prefer this to indemnity

3. **If NOT time-barred (<12 months):**
   - **Report to the lender before exchange** (HB 2.3)
   - The lender may require the borrower to obtain a regularisation certificate before completion
   - You likely **cannot certify good and marketable title** in this state

4. **Check the valuation:**
   - If the valuer valued the property assuming the loft conversion was approved, the valuation assumptions are incorrect — **report the discrepancy** to the lender (checks 3.1 and 3.2, HB 4.2–4.3)
   - The lender may require a revised valuation

5. **Check Part 2** for the specific lender's requirements on:
   - Acceptance of indemnity insurance for building regulations breaches
   - Whether they require a regularisation certificate instead of (or in addition to) indemnity
   - Any specific requirements about unauthorised structural alterations

## Summary of Required Actions

| Priority | Issue | Action | Handbook Ref |
|----------|-------|--------|-------------|
| **HIGH** | Lease term — 72 years | Check Part 2 for lender's minimum term. If fails: report before exchange. Consider lease extension. | HB 5.14.1 |
| **HIGH** | Loft conversion — no building regs | Establish date of works. If >12 months: arrange indemnity insurance. If <12 months: report to lender. In either case: check valuer's assumptions. | HB 5.5.1–5.5.2, 4.2–4.3 |
| **MEDIUM** | Valuation assumptions | Verify valuer was aware of (a) short lease term and (b) unauthorised loft conversion. Report any discrepancies. | HB 4.2–4.3 |
| **STANDARD** | Full leasehold checks | Complete all section 9 checks (forfeiture, ground rent, service charges, BSA 2022 etc.) | HB 5.14 |
| **STANDARD** | Indemnity insurance terms | If indemnity arranged: ensure lender named/noted, terms acceptable per Part 2 | HB 9 |

### The Golden Rule

> If you believe any matter should reasonably be considered important by the lender in deciding whether to lend, and you cannot disclose it due to conflict of interest, you must cease acting and return instructions *(HB 5.3.1)*.

Both a short lease and an unauthorised structural alteration are matters a reasonable lender would consider important. **Report both issues to the lender before exchange** unless you can fully resolve them (lease extension / indemnity insurance) and provide an unqualified Certificate of Title.

# Lender's Handbook Assessment: Short Lease & Loft Conversion Without Building Regs

## 1. Remaining Lease Term (72 Years)

The UK Finance Lender's Handbook Part 1 sets out requirements on unexpired lease terms. The key provisions are:

**Minimum term requirement:** Most lenders require that the lease has a minimum unexpired term at the *end* of the mortgage term — typically at least 30 years beyond the mortgage expiry. So for a 25-year mortgage, you'd generally need at least 55–60 years remaining at completion as an absolute floor, but many lenders set higher thresholds.

**The 80-year cliff:** At 72 years remaining, you are approaching the critical 80-year threshold below which the landlord can claim "marriage value" under the Leasehold Reform, Housing and Urban Development Act 1993. This significantly increases the cost of a future lease extension. Many lenders are aware of this and treat leases under 80 years with caution.

**Likely lender position at 72 years:**
- Some lenders will accept 72 years if it still exceeds their minimum unexpired term at end of mortgage (e.g., for a short mortgage term)
- Many mainstream lenders will flag this as insufficient or require a lease extension to be completed or formally committed to before completion
- Part 1, Section 5.14 (lease requirements) directs conveyancers to check the individual lender's Part 2 entry for their specific minimum unexpired term requirements — these vary significantly between lenders
- You **must** check the specific lender's Part 2 requirements for their minimum lease length

**Action required:** Check the lender's Part 2 handbook entry for their specific minimum unexpired lease term. Report to the lender if the lease falls below their stated minimum. Many lenders will require evidence that a lease extension is being pursued (either a Section 42 notice served, or a deed of variation agreed) before they will lend.

## 2. Loft Conversion Without Building Regulations Sign-Off

The Handbook addresses alterations and building work compliance under the property risk/structural sections.

**Part 1 requirements on building work:**
- Section 6.4 covers the conveyancer's duties regarding the property, including structural issues
- The Handbook requires that any structural alterations or building work comply with relevant planning permission and building regulations
- A loft conversion is structural work (involving roof structure, floor strengthening, fire safety, insulation, electrics, stairs) and **requires** building regulations approval under the Building Act 1984

**Missing building regulations sign-off means:**
- There is no completion certificate from Building Control (local authority or approved inspector)
- The work may not meet fire safety standards (critical for loft conversions — escape routes, fire doors, smoke detection)
- There may be structural deficiencies
- The local authority retains enforcement powers (though these are typically limited to 12 months from completion of work for a Section 36 injunction, the lack of sign-off remains a title issue)

**Likely lender position:**
- Most lenders will require either:
  1. A **building regulations completion certificate** (or final certificate from an approved inspector), OR
  2. A **regularisation certificate** obtained retrospectively from the local authority (under Section 36 of the Building Act 1984), OR
  3. An **indemnity insurance policy** covering the lack of building regulations approval — this is commonly accepted where the work was completed more than 12 months ago, but check the specific lender's Part 2 requirements
- The conveyancer must **report this to the lender** as it affects the property's value and the security for the mortgage
- Some lenders may also require a satisfactory surveyor's/structural engineer's report confirming the work is of adequate standard

**Key handbook obligation:** The conveyancer has a duty to report to the lender any matter that might affect the value of the property or the lender's security. A loft conversion without building regs sign-off falls squarely within this duty (Part 1, Section 6).

## Summary of Required Actions

| Issue | Handbook Section | Action Required |
|-------|-----------------|-----------------|
| 72-year lease | §5.14 (Leasehold) | Check lender's Part 2 for minimum unexpired term; likely need to report and may need lease extension commitment |
| Loft conversion — no building regs | §6.4 (Property) | Report to lender; obtain completion certificate, regularisation certificate, or indemnity insurance per lender's Part 2 requirements |

## Critical Notes

1. **Always check Part 2** — Part 1 sets the framework but each lender's Part 2 entry contains their specific requirements and thresholds. These vary significantly.
2. **Duty to report** — Both issues trigger the conveyancer's overriding duty to report matters affecting the lender's security. Failure to report could constitute a breach of the conveyancer's obligations under the Handbook and potentially give rise to a negligence claim.
3. **Combined risk** — Having *both* a short lease and unauthorised building work on the same property compounds the risk. The lender may take a stricter view when multiple issues are present.
4. **Indemnity insurance** — While commonly used for the building regs issue, ensure the lender accepts indemnity insurance as an alternative (most do, but check Part 2). Do NOT contact the local authority about the building regs issue before obtaining indemnity insurance, as this could invalidate the policy.
